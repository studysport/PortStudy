Measurements

Short Definition:

Measurement is the process of comparing a physical quantity with a standard unit to determine its magnitude.

Detailed Explanation:

Measurement is the foundation of physics because every scientific observation requires accurate numerical values. A physical quantity is anything that can be measured, such as length, mass, time, temperature, and electric current.

To measure a quantity, we compare it with a standard unit. The internationally accepted system of units is called the International System of Units (SI). In SI, the base units include meter (m) for length, kilogram (kg) for mass, second (s) for time, ampere (A) for electric current, kelvin (K) for temperature, mole (mol) for amount of substance, and candela (cd) for luminous intensity.

Physical quantities are divided into two types: fundamental quantities and derived quantities. Fundamental quantities are independent and not derived from other quantities, while derived quantities are obtained by combining fundamental quantities. For example, velocity is derived from length and time.

Accuracy refers to how close a measurement is to the true value, while precision describes how close repeated measurements are to each other. Significant figures are used to express the precision of measured values.

Errors in measurement can occur due to limitations of instruments or human mistakes. These errors may be systematic or random. Proper techniques and calibrated instruments help reduce errors.

Key Points:

  • Measurement compares quantity with a standard unit
  • SI system is internationally accepted
  • Includes fundamental and derived quantities
  • Accuracy and precision are important
  • Measurement errors can be systematic or random
