Vectors and Equilibrium

Short Definition:

A vector is a physical quantity that has both magnitude and direction.

Detailed Explanation:

In physics, quantities are divided into scalars and vectors. Scalars have only magnitude, such as mass, time, and temperature. Vectors have both magnitude and direction, such as velocity, acceleration, displacement, and force.

Vectors are represented by arrows. The length of the arrow shows magnitude, and the arrowhead shows direction. Vectors can be added or subtracted using graphical methods (head-to-tail rule) or analytical methods using components.

When two or more forces act on a body, their combined effect is called the resultant force. If the resultant force is zero, the body is said to be in equilibrium.

There are two types of equilibrium: translational equilibrium (net force is zero) and rotational equilibrium (net torque is zero). Conditions of equilibrium are important in engineering and structural design.

Key Points:

  • Vector has magnitude and direction
  • Scalar has only magnitude
  • Resultant force determines motion
  • Equilibrium means net force and torque are zero
