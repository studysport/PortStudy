Ionic Bond

Short Definition

An ionic bond is a chemical bond formed when one atom transfers electrons to another atom.

Detailed Explanation

An ionic bond is formed when electrons are transferred from one atom to another atom. This transfer usually occurs between a metal atom and a non-metal atom. When a metal atom loses electrons, it becomes a positively charged ion called a cation. When a non-metal atom gains electrons, it becomes a negatively charged ion called an anion.

The opposite charges of these ions attract each other and create a strong electrostatic force. This attractive force between oppositely charged ions forms an ionic bond. Because of this strong attraction, ionic compounds usually have high melting and boiling points.

One of the most common examples of an ionic compound is sodium chloride, also known as table salt. In this compound, sodium loses one electron and chlorine gains that electron. As a result, sodium becomes a positive ion and chlorine becomes a negative ion.

Ionic compounds usually form crystal structures and can conduct electricity when they are dissolved in water or melted. These properties make ionic compounds important in many industrial and chemical processes.
